# Concept Development: Periodization

*Is digital capitalism a new stage, or intensified late capitalism?*

**Status:** Draft  
**Last updated:** 2026-02-01

---

## The Core Question

What is the relationship between **digital capitalism** and earlier capitalist stages?

Options:
1. **New stage** — Qualitative break (like industrial capitalism from merchant capitalism)
2. **Intensification** — Quantitative acceleration of late capitalism
3. **Misdiagnosis** — No fundamental change; technology ≠ mode of production
4. **Tendency** — Emergent features that may or may not consolidate

---

## Historical Periodization (Standard Marxist)

| Stage | Period | Characteristics |
|-------|--------|-----------------|
| **Merchant Capitalism** | 16th-18th c. | Trade, colonialism, primitive accumulation |
| **Industrial Capitalism** | 18th-19th c. | Factory system, wage labor, machinery |
| **Monopoly/Imperialist Capitalism** | Late 19th-early 20th c. | Cartels, finance capital, imperialism |
| **Fordism** | 1945-1970s | Mass production/consumption, welfare state, unions |
| **Neoliberalism/Post-Fordism** | 1970s-present | Flexibility, financialization, globalization |
| **Digital Capitalism?** | 1990s-present | Platforms, data, AI, gig economy |

**Key question**: Is digital capitalism a sub-stage of neoliberalism, or successor?

---

## Position A: New Stage (Digital Capitalism)

### Key Proponents
- **Dan Schiller** — IT as new technological basis (like steam engine)
- **Christian Fuchs** — Systematic theory of digital capitalism as stage
- **Yann Moulier-Boutang** — Cognitive capitalism as successor to Fordism

### Criteria for New Stage

**1. New Technological Basis**
- Digital infrastructure as general condition of production
- Similar to steam engine in Industrial Revolution
- Schiller: "Networking the Global Market System"

**2. New Labor Process**
- Immaterial labor → different valorization
- Gig work, platform work → different exploitation
- Global digital division of labor

**3. New Accumulation Logic**
- Data extraction central (not just incidental)
- Rent becomes dominant (not just supplementary)
- Financialization intensifies

**4. New Class Composition**
- "Cognitariat" — knowledge workers
- Precariat — platform-dependent
- Vectoralist class (Wark) — controls information flows

**5. New Spatial Logic**
- "Space of flows" (Castells) replaces space of places
- Platform imperialism (Jin)
- Data colonialism (Couldry/Mejias)

### Key Texts
- Schiller, Dan. *Digital Capitalism* (1999)
- Fuchs, Christian. *Digital Capitalism* (2022)
- Moulier-Boutang, Yann. *Cognitive Capitalism* (2011)
- Castells, Manuel. *The Rise of the Network Society* (1996)

## Position B: Intensified Neoliberalism

### Key Proponents
- **David Harvey** — Continuation of accumulation by dispossession
- **Gérard Duménil / Dominique Lévy** — Neoliberalism as class project
- **Nancy Fraser** — Capitalism's "care" crisis intensified

### Arguments

**1. No New Technological Basis**
- Digital = extension of information technology (1940s-present)
- Just-in-time production, logistics = neoliberal innovations
- AI = continuation of automation trend

**2. Same Accumulation Logic**
- Still wage labor + surplus value
- Still primitive accumulation (enclosure)
- Still financialization

**3. Political Continuity**
- Neoliberalism since 1970s
- Platform capitalism = neoliberal platform governance
- No new class compromise (like Fordism)

**4. Critique of "New Stage" Theses**
- Technological determinism
- Ignores continuity of exploitation
- Overstates difference

### Key Texts
- Harvey, David. *A Brief History of Neoliberalism* (2005)
- Harvey, David. *The New Imperialism* (2003)
- Duménil, Gérard and Lévy, Dominique. *The Crisis of Neoliberalism* (2011)

---

## Position C: Misdiagnosis (No Fundamental Change)

### Key Proponents
- **George Caffentzis** — "Cognitive capitalism is science fiction"
- **Heesang Jeon** — Value theory persists
- **Michael Heinrich** — Value theory critique of "new stages"

### Arguments

**1. Capitalism Remains Capitalism**
- M-C-M' still the formula
- Abstract labor still value substance
- Class struggle still motor of history

**2. Technology ≠ Mode of Production**
- Steam engine didn't change capitalism's essence
- Neither does digital technology
- Mode of production = property relations, not tools

**3. Critique of Post-Workerism**
- "End of work" = ideological
- "General intellect" misreads Marx
- Immaterial labor still material

**4. Political Danger**
- "New stage" = abandoning class analysis
- "Cognitariat" = middle-class ideology
- Need worker struggle, not "multitude"

### Key Texts
- Caffentzis, George. "Immeasurable Value?" (2005)
- Jeon, Heesang. "Cognitive Capitalism or Cognition in Capitalism?" (2010)
- Heinrich, Michael. *An Introduction to the Three Volumes of Karl Marx's Capital* (2004)

---

## Position D: Tendency (Not Yet Consolidated)

### Key Proponents
- **Nick Srnicek** — "Tendencies" language; platform logic emerging
- **McKenzie Wark** — "Vectoral" power rising but contested
- **Various autonomist thinkers**

### Arguments

**1. Emergent Features**
- Platforms show new possibilities
- Not yet hegemonic
- Contingent on struggle outcomes

**2. Uneven Development**
- Some sectors digitalized (media, logistics)
- Others not (agriculture, manufacturing)
- Geographic variation (Global North vs. South)

**3. Open Historical Process**
- Could consolidate into new stage
- Could be reabsorbed into old logic
- Depends on class struggle

**4. Strategic Implication**
- Don't assume inevitability
- Contested terrain
- Intervention possible

### Key Texts
- Srnicek, Nick. *Platform Capitalism* (2017)
- Wark, McKenzie. *Capital Is Dead* (2019)
- Lazzarato, Maurizio. *Signs and Machines* (2014)

## Comparative Assessment

| Criterion | New Stage (A) | Intensification (B) | No Change (C) | Tendency (D) |
|-----------|---------------|---------------------|---------------|--------------|
| **New tech basis?** | Yes | Partial | No | Emerging |
| **New labor process?** | Yes | Quantitative | No | Partial |
| **New accumulation logic?** | Yes (data/rent) | No | No | Possible |
| **New class composition?** | Yes | No | No | Possible |
| **Empirical fit** | Good for tech/media | Good for global | Good for persistence | Good for contingency |
| **Political utility** | Structural analysis | Class continuity | Worker focus | Open strategy |

## Our Working Hypothesis

*Tentative synthesis:*

Digital capitalism is best understood as **"intensified neoliberalism with emergent tendencies toward a new stage"**:

**Continuities with Neoliberalism (B, C):**
- Wage labor and surplus value remain central
- Financialization deepens rather than transforms
- Primitive accumulation continues (data enclosure)
- Class structure: capital vs. labor still fundamental

**Emergent Tendencies toward New Stage (A, D):**
- Data extraction becoming systematic (not incidental)
- Platform monopoly creating new spatial logic
- Rent potentially becoming dominant valorization mode
- Global digital division of labor restructuring North-South relations

**Contingency (D):**
- Whether these tendencies consolidate = political question
- Class struggle will determine outcome
- Not technologically determined

**Periodization proposal:**

| Period | Dominant Feature |
|--------|------------------|
| 1945-1973 | Fordism |
| 1973-1995 | Neoliberalism (early) |
| 1995-2007 | Neoliberalism (digital emergence) |
| 2007-present | Platform capitalism (consolidation?) |
| Future? | ??? (contested) |

Key dates:
- **1995**: Browser commercialization (Netscape)
- **2007**: Smartphone (iPhone); financial crisis
- **2020s**: AI/LLM emergence; platform regulation attempts

## Empirical Indicators

What would confirm "new stage" thesis?

1. **Macroeconomic**
   - Rent > profit in aggregate surplus?
   - Data sectors drive growth?
   - New long-wave Kondratiev cycle?

2. **Labor market**
   - Platform work > traditional employment?
   - "Cognitive" labor becomes majority?
   - Global digital division institutionalized?

3. **Political**
   - Platform governance replaces state functions?
   - New regulatory frameworks (not just antitrust)?
   - "Digital rights" as new political axis?

4. **Ideological**
   - Platform capitalism legitimated as "new economy"?
   - Post-work/post-scarcity discourse dominant?
   - Traditional worker identity eroded?

## Strategic Implications

### If New Stage (A)
- Need new organizational forms
- Unions insufficient; platforms don't fit industrial model
- "Exodus" strategies (Terranova, etc.)

### If Intensification (B)
- Traditional worker organizing still relevant
- Anti-imperialist frameworks apply
- Class struggle continues in familiar forms

### If No Change (C)
- Orthodox Marxism sufficient
- Focus on workplace struggle
- Reject "new social movements"

### If Tendency (D)
- Strategic flexibility needed
- Multiple forms of struggle
- Contingent interventions

**Our position (tentative):** D with elements of B
- Don't assume inevitability of any outcome
- Multiple forms of struggle needed
- Both traditional worker organizing AND new platform-specific tactics
